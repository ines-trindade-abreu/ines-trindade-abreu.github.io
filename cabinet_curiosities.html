What Lives Here

This cabinet holds the curious, the unfinished, and the deeply interesting. My research sits at the boundary between the exact sciences and the humanities — between the nucleus of an atom and the network of people who first named it, measured it, and argued over what it meant.

At Técnico Lisboa I have built a foundation in nuclear and radiation physics, with a particular interest in their applications to health and environmental sciences. In parallel, I have found myself increasingly drawn to the history and sociology of science: how knowledge is produced, who gets credit, and how ideas travel through communities of scholars across time.

My MSc thesis brings these two worlds together, applying network analysis to the early radioactivity community — a moment in history when the discipline was being invented, the rules were still being written, and some of the most important contributors were women navigating a deeply unequal field.

Current Work

MSc Thesis

Active Network Science History of Science Nuclear Physics

"At the Origin Node: A Temporal Network Analysis of Knowledge Diffusion in the Radioactivity Community, 1898–1938"

MSc in Engineering Physics · Instituto Superior Técnico, Universidade de Lisboa · Pending approval

How does scientific knowledge spread through a community of scholars? Who were the gatekeepers, the bridges, the early adopters — and what role did gender play in shaping who was cited, who was read, and who was forgotten?

This thesis constructs and analyses a temporal citation network of the early radioactivity community between 1898 and 1938 — from Becquerel's discovery through to the onset of nuclear fission. Using tools from network science (centrality measures, community detection, temporal analysis), the work traces how knowledge about radioactivity diffused through an international scholarly network, with particular attention to the structural position and influence of Marie Curie and other women researchers in a field that was simultaneously being defined and gatekept.

The project draws on digitised historical literature, bibliometric databases, and computational methods in Python, and is situated at the intersection of physics, history, and the sociology of science.
